Impacts of Plastic Pollution on Biodiversity

Take a guess... How long does it take to decompose plastic?

Although only only 3% of the waste in the landfill is plastic waste (Australia Bureau of Statistics, 2020), it can take up to 450 years to decompose a plastic bag (Goodfor Company, n.d.). Additionally, less than 10% of the consumed plastic was recycled (McLaren, 2021). The low recycling rate means there was a high proportion sent to landfill, or was left on the land and ocean.

Have you ever seen any plastic bottles when walking on the beach? Where do you think they came from? It’s common to see plastic floating in the ocean and no one may report it. Thus, the amount of plastic in surface waters is not very well known. However, in estimation, the amount ranges from 10,000 to 100,000 tonnes (Our World In Data, n.d.). You may wonder... Why does it matter?

Endangered aquatic species are threatened with extinction

There are aquatic species’ (animals that live in water) population decreasing at an alarming rate and being considered endangered (facing a very high risk of extinction). Fishes are the only animal groups that have over 3000 species threatened with extinction. Numerous causes devastate their population, such as climate change and water pollution. Plastic pollution is threatening their size as it degrades the water quality (IUCN, 2022).

Most of the threatened aquatic species' population is decreasing

Due to plastic waste being emitted to the ocean, the loggerhead sea turtles (the world's largest hard-shelled turtle) are listed as endangered. Additionally, according to Condor Ferries (n.d.), over 1 million seabirds and 100,000 marine species die from plastic pollution every year. Plastic has been found in more than 60% of all seabirds and in 100% of sea turtles species that mistake plastic for food (Ocean Conservancy, n.d.).

Besides that, there are a number of whales threatened by plastic pollution, such as blue whales, the largest animal known to ever exist. How are they affected? ABC news (2018) reported that a sperm whale was washed up on a beach in Spain and died due to nearly 30 kg of trash, including plastic, that blocked its digestive system.

About 70% of deep sea fishes mistake plastic for food

WWF revealed that 70% of deep-sea fish (e.g. tuna, grouper & shark) have eaten plastic. Furthermore, it was proved that some popular dinner time favourites (e.g. oysters, mussels & sardines) consumed plastic when they were at the fisheries.

Marine species are entangled by plastic debris

Apart from mistaking plastic for food, some marine species are entangled by plastic, especially abandoned fishing gear. Those species cannot breathe when they are entangled in plastic. Then, the entanglement kills them through drowning.

Plastic waste is also impacting the whole ecosystem

Plastic releases greenhouse gases during the manufacturing and biodegrading process. Greenhouse gases may increase the frequency of extreme weather, which threatens not only marine life but also the whole ecosystem and us.

How is this affecting us?

An increasing demand for seafood worldwide

As you can see from the data below, our demand for seafood has been increasing throughout the past thirty years. Undoubtedly, this uptrend will continue in the future. Besides, we should notice this as a signal that we have to keep the marine clean and safe. It is because there is evidence that microplastics and nanoplastics (tiny, almost undetectable, pieces of plastic) can move from a fish’s stomach to its muscle tissue, which is the part that we typically eat (McInturf & Savoca, 2019).

2020

As we are subjected to long-term exposure to nanoplastics at low concentrations, it is hard to tell the impact of consuming fish with little nanoplastics inside. However, a study showed that excessive nanoplastics intake will destroy our cell membrane structure leading to cell death (Lai et al, 2022). Also, it may accumulate in different organs and tissues and may induce in the long term the development of cancer (Domenech et al, 2023).

So, what can we do?

Everyone can do something to help solve the plastic pollution problem, and a lot of people worldwide are already taking action to lower their plastic use. It’s not necessary to spend any to help address the plastic waste issue. Waste minimization can reduce plastic waste emitted to the ocean, which protects the biodiversity of marine species. We can achieve this by focusing on the first of the 3Rs (reducing, reusing and recycling).
