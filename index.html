Impacts of Plastic Pollution on Biodiversity

Take a guess... How long does it take to decompose plastic?

The Australia Bureau of Statistics shows that only 3% of the waste in the landfill is plastic waste. However, as suggested by Goodfor Company, it can take up to 450 years to decompose a plastic bag. Additionally, less than 10% of the consumed plastic was recycled (McLaren). The low recycling rate means there was a high proportion sent to landfill, or was left on the land and ocean.

Have you ever seen any plastic bottles when walking on the beach? Where do you think they came from? It’s common to see plastic floating in the ocean but no one may report it. Thus, the amount of plastic in surface waters is not very well known. However, in estimation, Our World In Data reported that the amount ranges from 10,000 to 100,000 tonnes. You may wonder... Why does it matter?

Endangered aquatic species are threatened with extinction

There are aquatic species’ (animals that live in water) populations decreasing at an alarming rate and being considered endangered (facing a very high risk of extinction). “Fishes” is the only animal group that has over 3000 endangered animals. Numerous causes devastate their population, such as climate change and water pollution. IUCN revealed that Plastic pollution is threatening their size as it degrades the water quality.

The data from 2012 to 2021 was collected and used linear regression to predict the potential number of threatened species in the future. The predictions are mostly lining up with the actual value in 2022. However, most of them have underestimated the actual value in 2022. Thus, we should consider this, especially since the amount of threatened species has a high risk of rising.

Number of species threatened with extinction (Animals)

Most of the threatened aquatic species' population is decreasing

Due to plastic waste being emitted to the ocean, the loggerhead sea turtles (the world's largest hard-shelled turtle) are listed as endangered. Additionally, according to Condor Ferries, over 1 million seabirds and 100,000 marine species die from plastic pollution every year. Ocean Conservancy added that plastic has been found in more than 60% of all seabirds and in 100% of sea turtles species that mistake plastic for food.

Marine species threatened by waste pollution (Population trend)

Besides that, there are a number of whales threatened by plastic pollution, such as blue whales, the largest animal known to ever exist. How are they affected? ABC news reported that a sperm whale was washed up on a beach in Spain and died due to nearly 30 kg of trash, including plastic, that blocked its digestive system.

About 70% of deep sea fishes mistake plastic for food

WWF revealed that 70% of deep-sea fish (e.g. tuna, grouper & shark) have eaten plastic. Furthermore, it was proved that some popular dinner time favourites (e.g. oysters, mussels & sardines) consumed plastic when they were at the fisheries.

Marine species are entangled by plastic debris

Apart from mistaking plastic for food, some marine species are entangled by plastic, especially abandoned fishing gear. Those species cannot breathe when they are entangled in plastic. Then, the entanglement kills them through drowning.

Plastic waste is also impacting the whole ecosystem

Plastic releases greenhouse gases during the manufacturing and biodegrading process. Greenhouse gases may increase the frequency of extreme weather, which threatens not only marine life but also the whole ecosystem and us.

How is this affecting us?

An increasing demand for seafood worldwide

As you can see from the data below, our demand for seafood has been increasing throughout the past thirty years. Undoubtedly, this uptrend will continue in the future. Besides, we should notice this as a signal that we have to keep the marine clean and safe. It is because there is evidence that microplastics and nanoplastics (tiny, almost undetectable, pieces of plastic) can move from a fish’s stomach to its muscle tissue. McInturf & Savoca mentioned that this is the part that we typically eat.

As we are subjected to long-term exposure to nanoplastics at low concentrations, it is hard to tell the impact of consuming fish with little nanoplastics inside. However, Lai et al. study showed that excessive nanoplastics intake will destroy our cell membrane structure leading to cell death. Also, Domenech et al. reported that it may accumulate in different organs and tissues and may induce in the long term the development of cancer.

So, what can we do?

Everyone can do something to help solve the plastic pollution problem, and a lot of people worldwide are already taking action to lower their plastic use. It’s not necessary to spend any to help address the plastic waste issue. Waste minimization can reduce plastic waste emitted to the ocean, which protects the biodiversity of marine species. We can achieve this by focusing on the first of the 3Rs (reducing, reusing and recycling).

Using sustainable alternatives to single-use plastics

Plastic products are a convenient option whether it is on the go or at home. However, these products can be problematic when it comes to their disposal. To reduce the amount of plastic consumed, opt for alternative products with longer life cycles.
